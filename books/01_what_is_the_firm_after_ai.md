## Core Question

What is the firm after AI?

The traditional question asks why firms exist.

This book asks a sharper question:

> What is the firm when complex coordination capability can be generated, accumulated, invoked, replicated, and controlled beyond the traditional firm boundary?

---

## Core Answer

A firm is not merely a legal entity, a contract bundle, a production function, or a management hierarchy.

A firm is a structure for the stable control of complex coordination capability.

AI matters because it can change where that capability is generated, accumulated, invoked, replicated, controlled, and made accountable.

---

## Main Argument

Before AI, many firms could assume that key organizational capabilities were mostly embedded inside their employees, processes, routines, culture, and management systems.

After AI, that assumption becomes weaker.

Capability may now be partially generated or amplified by:

- AI models
- external platforms
- vendors
- workflow systems
- data-feedback loops
- employee-machine combinations
- customer interaction systems
- hybrid operational networks

The firm therefore becomes harder to define by legal boundary alone.

The real question becomes:

> Can the firm still stabilize, control, reproduce, and take responsibility for the capability on which it depends?

## What the Book Does Not Claim

This book does not claim that firms will disappear.

It does not claim that AI replaces all human organization.

It does not claim that legal entities no longer matter.

It argues that the legal form of the firm is no longer sufficient to explain where organizational capability is actually produced and controlled.

---

## One-Sentence Summary

After AI, the firm must be understood as a structure for controlling complex coordination capability, not merely as a legal container for assets, contracts, employees, and profit.
